Rhetorical Analysis: Apple's "A Day in the Life of Your Data"

Apple's Strategic Position on Data Privacy

In the larger discourse on big data security and privacy, Apple Inc stands out as a major proponent of protective measures for user generated personal data. This overt promotion of Apple's privacy rhetoric is intentional. Growing distrust of technology companies is a concern for corporate leadership in terms of profitability and continued growth, prompting an aspirational appearance positioning Apple as a privacy champion that users can trust with their data. Recent regulatory shifts prompted by big data misuse such as the Cambridge Analytica scandal have resulted in governmental intervention including the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA), California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), and California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA). Apple's increasingly privacy driven advertising campaigns are one such example of regulatory and public opinion shaping the rhetoric of Apple in regard to big data utilization.

The White Paper as Rhetorical Artifact

The rhetorical artifact at the center of this analysis is a white paper titled "A Day in the Life of Your Data." The rhetoric espoused in this consumer-facing document is no doubt the reaction to situational aspects and shifting ideologies regarding data privacy. Apple has made increasing efforts to launch privacy centered features across their entire product catalogue. There are a multitude of reasons for which Apple's position has shifted in regard to big data. Arguably, Apple has played an enormous role in the expansion of big data use by offering smart phones that millions of individuals around the world carry in their pocket from day to day collecting thousands of data points on individuals. Additionally, as seen in recent privacy forward advertising campaigns from prominent consumer technology brands, privacy washing can be seen as an effort to build trust with consumers and a strategy to curb doubts about big data security. Not only that, but Apple's privacy forward brand image and release of app tracking transparency in 2021 garnered the tech giant even more control over big data generated by IOS users. By disguising their decision making with rhetorical misdirection, Apple crafts a message that promotes their image while bringing to the forefront the reality of big data use in advertising and other technological efficiencies.

Document Overview: Demystifying Big Data

"A Day in the Life of Your Data" is an attempt to demystify the various interactions that lead to personal data collection by focusing on a father-daughter day at the playground. By framing their rhetoric within an innocent day spent with family, Apple depicts the lack of awareness that average consumers have when it comes to personal data collection. Throughout the course of this white paper, it is evident that Apple used a variety of rhetorical appeals to position themselves as champions of privacy. The proceeding analysis will cover the following topics: the use of kairos and the rhetorical situation prompting such a rhetorical artifact, emotional appeals to personalize the issue, appeals to credibility of Apple as an organization of principles upholding fundamental human rights, misdirection to position advertisers as the primary culprits of big data misuse, and logical appeals through data as rhetoric.

Kairos and the Rhetorical Situation

To understand the rhetorical choices made by Apple in "A Day in the Life of Your Data" close attention must be paid to the rhetorical situation that frames it. Like any major corporation, especially big tech companies, priorities lie with stakeholder confidence. In the wake of the numerous big data scandals across the 2010s, everything from Cambridge Analytica to Whistleblower Edward Snowden's release of surveillance documents from the National Security Agency (NSA), Apple knew that data privacy was at the top of many individuals minds. So, Apple took a strategic approach to position themselves as data privacy champions while simultaneously undercutting big tech competition. One such example of Apple's data privacy persona can be found in its marketing campaign better known as "Privacy. That's iPhone."

"Privacy. That's iPhone" Campaign

In a one-minute video posted to YouTube in 2022 (iFans), a man named Felix is presented with a cup of coffee that bares his name. Upon receiving his coffee from the barista and making his way out of the coffee shop, the barista jumps the counter and begins to follow Felix, going so far as to join Felix in his Taxi. Then, the message becomes clear. Upon interacting with the Taxi driver who asks "name?" the barista quickly responds with Felix's name followed by his date of birth. What we can see here is a metaphorical representation of the way big data flourishes, through aggregate data tracked across a multitude of digital technologies. The video titled "Privacy on iPhone" then goes on to offer increasingly absurd representations of a growing mob of individuals that represent each instance of data sharing among different entities. By the end of the short advertisement a distressed Felix turns to his iPhone and enables the newly released app tracking transparency feature that allows IOS users to "Ask App Not to Track." This is followed by each of the individuals that represent particular instances of unintended data sharing to vanish with a puff of air. A soundtrack repeating the mantra "why don't you mind your own business" in a melodic rhythm becomes increasingly noticeable and Apple's revitalized position on big data proudly displays "Privacy. That's iPhone."

Safari and Surveillance Rhetoric

More recently in 2024 (Apple), Apple released a short advertisement for their signature browser called Safari. The advertisement shows an increased disdain toward trackers of big data, vilifying them as representations of a fringe surveillance conspiracy theory better known as "birds aren't real." We can see progression from metaphorically representing data trackers as annoying humans that monitor your data with each interaction to a flock of birds whose bodies mimic the shape of stereotypical surveillance cameras. The video follows numerous seemingly ordinary individuals using their iPhone for various tasks that require web browsing. Ominously, the phrase "Your browsing is being watched" appears on screen as the flock of surveillance birds grows increasingly intrusive in the lives of the individuals depicted earlier. In the last 15 seconds of the video, a young woman sees the flock flying towards her. She opens her Safari browser, and the surveillance birds begin to explode one by one in a montage of other users opening Safari. The screen displays: "Safari. A browser that's actually private" followed by "Privacy. That's iPhone."

White Paper Context and Timeline

While the two marketing examples have presented a clear picture of how Apple rhetorically positions themselves at data privacy champions to their audiences, "A Day in the Life of Your Data" was published prior to the release of Apple's notable push towards data privacy in there marketing campaigns and brand image. Dated April 2021 amidst the Covid-19 pandemic, Apple released this rhetorical artifact prior to the launch of their new App Tracking Transparency feature metaphorically depicted in later marketing campaigns. The white paper is a 13 page document rife with imagery and statistics but generally follows the structure of demystifying the various data processes that occur in an average father-daughter day at the playground, Apple's privacy principles and existing features that give users control over their data, promotion of their App Tracking Transparency feature, and a breakdown of the process that makes up digital advertising and its reliance on personal data.

Emotional Appeals: Family and Vulnerability

Throughout this document, imagery of a father and daughter enjoying their time together is ever-present. One such representation of John and Emma shows John gazing in the distance imagining himself pushing his daughter on a swing set while his computer in the background collects data on the various interactions that the father made while planning the day. This is followed by numerous instances of the father-daughter duo enjoying their day together while technologies that track their data linger in the background. By doing this, Apple appeals to the emotion of their audiences where a seemingly innocent family is encroached upon to the lack of their knowledge. This rhetorical ploy initially positions all collectors of data as nefarious while uplifting Apple as an entity of transparency and upholder of human dignity. Apple ends the emotional appeal section by stating "At the end of the day, a number of companies John has never interacted with, all around the world, have updated their profiles with information about him and his daughter" (Apple 6).

Credibility Through Human Rights

After describing how big data operates through the emotional representation of an unsuspecting family, Apple firmly presents their principals on privacy. "Apple believes that privacy is a fundamental human right" initiates this rhetorical strategy that distinguishes Apple as a credible harbinger of human dignity (7). By positioning themselves as an object of principle, Apple reinforces the idea that they are the protectors of your data despite only giving IOS users the agency to choose how their data is tracked as recently as 2021.

Existing Features and Track Record

Following Apple's privacy principles is a list of preexisting Apple features that give John more transparency and control over his data. The proceeding structure compartmentalizes each section of John and Emma's day with annotations describing various features that John could have enabled to prevent data collection from outside sources. This ploy positions John as the individual responsible to protect his data and to utilize Apple features to meet those goals. As these features were existing at the time of this white papers publication, Apple transfers the message that they have a track record of success and offer the solution to problems surrounding big data usage. This track record of success rhetorically adds to the narrative that Apple constructs around data privacy.

App Tracking Transparency: The Next Step

After that, Apple doubles down to present a feature that is said to "…take the next step to protect users' privacy within the app ecosystem" (9). This marks a key distinction in time where Apple took a strategic approach to position themselves as data privacy champions while simultaneously undercutting big tech competition who gladly took advantage of the big data that was accessible through IOS. Apple then acknowledges "previously opaque and hidden" practices around personal data use while committing to developing "innovative privacy technologies" and to "keep your information safe" (9). Apple offers us some humility by acknowledging that their innovations have become a prominent vector of big data collection while simultaneously realigning their mission to make privacy their central concern.

Misdirection: Advertisers as Culprits

Lastly, Apple reinforces the idea that other entities are responsible for the mishandling of user data by describing "A Day in the Life of an Ad" where ad auction processes happen in real time where advertisers sell, collect, and exchange personal data at a massive scale. Apple also goes into ad attribution, a key player in the use of big data, where user data is tracked across all possible interactions with technology such as between the web, apps, and offline interactions. This misdirection once again positions Apple as the hero while all other parties involved are the once who take advantage of user data.

Data as Rhetoric: The Power of Numbers

What is interesting about the rhetoric of big data is that data itself has a rhetorical dimension. Through the course of this white paper, facts about big data usage can be found on the margins. One fact reads: "Hundreds of data brokers harvest online and offline data. One broker collects data on 700 million consumers worldwide creating consumer profiles with up to 5,000 characteristics" (Apple 4). This data creates a logical appeal pointing to the scale of the issue and builds Apples credibility by representing them as an organization that deals in facts. People tend to trust data. We refer to polls to see which political candidate has the most public favor and our entire belief systems and ideologies often hinge on data thanks to revolutions in science and technology. It is interesting to consider that rhetoric about big data can contain data itself. A major reason that personal data is used at massive scale is the clarity that it offers regarding ad attribution and understanding users. In the same way, data is used in a rhetorical dimension to appeal to logic.
