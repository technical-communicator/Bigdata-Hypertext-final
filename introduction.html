Introduction

In the past 15 years, big data has become an ever-growing term in the vernacular of individuals somewhat engaged with digital technologies. But what is big data? Aside from recent events highlighting the potential negative and positive outcomes of its utilization, big data is just that. Big. As time goes by it is increasingly harder to avoid the use of digital technologies to complete everyday tasks. Interactions such as debit or credit card, mobile application use, and virtually all use of digital products includes a caveat, your personal data is bought and sold in real time at any given moment of the day. Yes, any time a user interacts with some form of digital technology, data is collected, but data is nothing new. We have used data to track information for an exceptionally long time, much longer than the advent of digital technology. With the widespread proliferation of digital technologies taking more of our time and attention, the sheer scale of data has become something of a resource. Most if not all major corporations benefit from the collection of user data. Not only that, but non-tech companies that have adapted to our digital revolution effectively have become prominent players in the ecosystem that enables big data. Innovation, security, targeted advertising, you name it, large scale data has been championed as the next frontier of our economy and the mecca of progress. But not everyone feels this way.

Big data is supported and opposed by every facet of society, from our elected representatives, major corporations, and the discourse of daily life. How entities are persuaded to take a stance on the use of big data is highly relevant and predominantly espoused by rhetoric. The rhetoric of technology is nothing new. As a country that has benefited massively off industrialization and technological innovation, the United States is no stranger to the rhetoric of technology. Many of our largest technological companies are fueled by shareholder confidence and the promise of unrestricted growth. Therefore, rhetoric of technology is inherently attached to neoliberal ideologies of unrestricted economic growth. That being said, much of the rhetoric around the idea of big data is positioned as overly positive in the grand scheme of innovation. But not everyone feels this way. Many concerns about privacy and surveillance have been voiced by rhetors large and small, pointing to the duality of the topic. Organizations that rely on big data often position themselves through the use of rhetoric to justify such use; their reasoning can vary tremendously but it is certain that the aim to target their audience by appealing to credibility, emotion, and logic.

There are a multitude of rhetorical artifacts which carry the persuasive and rhetorical strategies found in the discourse around big data. As key beneficiaries of big data, large technology companies have been increasingly vocal as a way to reassure their customers and stakeholders. A company that has driven the wide scale adoption of smartphone technology which tracks more data than most would believe, paints a favorable image of themselves through rhetorical choices. In marketing and advertising spheres, Apple Inc presents itself as a privacy forward technology company in an effort to build trust with consumers. In this rhetorical analysis, a white paper titled "A Day in the Life of Your Data" along with examples from Apple's "Privacy. That's Apple" marketing campaign are examined to shed light on the rhetorical strategies used to position Apple as a champion of big data security.

This paper attempts to highlight the rhetoric of big data and the various kairotic events which have forced major players to come to terms with big data and outwardly portray themselves to their audiences through the use of rhetoric. First, to express the rhetorical landscape around big data, this paper highlights the technical basis of big data, what scholars have found studying big data rhetorically, the various positions taken by proponents and opponents of big data, and the rhetorical situation that frames all discourse on the topic. Then, by examining the rhetoric found in multiple artifacts from Apple Inc on their position on big data, we will see how Apple positions itself to their audiences, the rhetorical situation that prompted such an artifact, the design choices that encompass the rhetoric used, the rhetorical strategies used to persuade audiences, and how the rhetoric of the artifact is situated in the larger discussion around big data.

Apple's kairotic approach to positioning itself as a privacy champion is done through emotional appeals which display data privacy as a fundamental human right, while distinguishing itself as entirely different from the entities that benefit from the collection and utilization of big data. Meanwhile Apple's "A Day in the Life of Your Data" presents a new feature to disrupt the entire big data ecosystem, solidifying their hold as a dominating big tech company. This rhetoric presents a picture that magnifies user agency while simultaneously building an ethos unlike large scale competitors fraught with data breach scandals. Apple crafted their message with consideration of kairos to deliver their message of data privacy in a rhetorical situation most favorable to them.
