The Rhetorical Landscape: Innovation, Surveillance, and Public Trust

Defining Big Data

As mentioned, data is not a new concept, in fact data can be traced all the way back to archaic evidence of humans counting and tracking items. Data is defined by Montag and Baumeister as information that can be used for calculating and reasoning (444). What makes big data different is the scale in which data is being collected through digital interactions. Additionally, big data is data that is cloud based, real-time, and unstructured data that is sourced from decentralized entities and IOT devices (Montag and Baumeister 444). Anticipation of the benefits of big data are overwhelmingly positive, but the topic has become increasingly contentious in a variety of domains. Big data breaches and scandals have fueled public outcry towards the increasing vulnerability of all parties involved with data, whether that be the people whose data is collected or the collectors of data. One such example is Cambridge Analytica. In "Oversharing the Super Safe Stuff: Privacy Washing in Apple iPhone and Google Pixel Commercials" Cirucci writes about Cambridge Analytica's use of aggregate data to train algorithms used to take advantage of people by targeting their personal data and networks (10). To add to that, Mehlenbacher and Mehlenbacher, argue that expert appeals on the grounds of technical specialism can work to direct attention towards logos and away from ethos as was done in the arguments around Cambridge Analytica effort to persuade voters from the use of personal data extracted from big data aggregate (22).

Big Data as a Scholarly Concern

While big data use has increasingly concerned the minds of users, big data has become a prominent staple for the success of big technology companies. This resulted in a growing number of entities persuading audience members of their stances on the concept of big data. Scholars have written extensively on the rhetoric of big data (Adamczyk, Bernal, Bygrave, Cirucci) but also data as rhetoric (Saifer and Dacin). Since data itself is used to impart logical appeals on audiences, an interesting theme is present through the holistic analysis of rhetoric used around big data.

Historical and Mythological Narratives

An article by Adamczyk investigates big data through a narrative based historical lens identifying the Middle-Landscape Myth as a prominent narrative that shapes the rhetoric of big data. By attributing big data rhetoric to a narrative framework, we see that widely acceptive narrative structures take on greater importance than logic (Adamczyk 5). Adamczyk also adds that due to the digital nature of big data infrastructure, big data technologies are easily attached to myth an narratives of the American frontier where big data becomes an untapped resource to be exploited (11). In "Communicating Dataism" Adamczyk argues that big data rhetoric is rooted in technoliberalism and technoutopianism where ideologies keep intact a generally positive view on big data where large quantities of unused data are utilized for economic progress (7).

Cybersecurity and Regulatory Discourse

In terms of cyber security issues surrounding big data, in "The Cultural Construction of Cyber Security: Digital Threats and Dangerous Rhetoric" Bernal conducted a discourse analysis to examine the rise of militaristic rhetoric from news, government officials, and corporate experts. This representation of big data threatens as grounds for national security and cyber warfare justifications for big data use and surveillance (625). Building on that idea, Bygrave claims that aspirational cyber security initiatives such as the Security by Design rhetoric could still position big data protection as a regulatory principle that could be weakened if used to further authoritarian or corporate interests at the expense of civil liberties (128). In an analysis of Apple's and Google's privacy washing marketing campaigns in comparison to their data privacy policies, Cirucci found that companies purposely conflate the concept of privacy with the security narrative described by Bygrave. Conflicting definitions of privacy and security across big tech companies policies and advertisements leaves aggregate data and inferred data as something separate from personal data (Cirucci 10).

Data as a Rhetorical Object

Lastly, in "Data and Organization Studies: Aesthetics, Emotions, Discourse and Our Everyday Encounters with Data" Saifer and Dacin cite the overall fetishization of data as a means to justify big data use and data as a means of persuasive rhetoric (632). Fetishization of data stretches further into rhetorical justifications of both control and freedom (Ortega 6). Ortega also investigates the rhetoric of big data in terms of dystopian fantasies constituted by surveillance and utopian fantasies constituted by innovation (7). This was framed by an understanding of the increasing focuses of techno regulatory imaginary that positions big data as future oriented and anticipatory (Rommetveit and Dijk 13).
